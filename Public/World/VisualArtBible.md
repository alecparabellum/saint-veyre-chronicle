# Saint Veyre Visual Art Bible

## Purpose

This document defines the visual direction for all public-facing imagery in the Saint Veyre Chronicle: characters, locations, factions, institutions, chapter and session covers, handouts, objects, clues, and relics.

The goal is unity without visual cloning. Images should clearly inhabit the same world while preserving strong differences between subjects.

## Core Style

**Cinematic painterly realism.**

Images should combine believable anatomy, faces, materials, architecture, and light with a polished painted finish. Brushwork should remain visible but controlled. Detail should be concentrated around the narrative focal point, especially faces, hands, significant objects, and readable evidence.

Skin should retain natural texture and tonal variation. Materials must feel distinct: velvet, old paper, rain-dark stone, polished glass, steel, brass, leather, wood, concrete, and stained glass should not collapse into the same generic surface.

## Supernatural Visibility

The supernatural is usually subtle and plausible.

Routine images may suggest vampirism through unnatural stillness, unusual eye reflections, slightly cold skin tones, disproportionate charisma, deep architectural shadows, or reflections that feel faintly wrong.

Overt supernatural imagery is reserved for frenzy, feeding, Discipline use, prophecy, transformation, major revelation, Masquerade failure, or climactic chapter art.

## Shared Saint Veyre Language

Consistency comes from three pillars:

1. Painterly rendering and material realism.
2. Motivated cinematic lighting and controlled shadow.
3. Period-meets-modern production design.

Saint Veyre should repeatedly juxtapose inherited grandeur, industrial residue, and contemporary wealth. Examples include Gothic stone behind security glass, antique furniture beside current technology, LED light reflected across carved masonry, luxury tailoring inside converted factories, and modern offices occupying old civic or industrial buildings.

There is no mandatory campaign-wide palette. Characters, factions, locations, and chapters receive their own palettes.

## Lighting

Light must come from a believable source. Appropriate sources include chandeliers, antique sconces, moonlight through tall windows, sodium streetlights, theatre spotlights, industrial work lamps, stained glass, desk lamps, corporate LEDs, and neon diffused by fog or rain.

Shadows should remain deep but readable. Avoid applying identical blue-black darkness to every image.

## Character Portraits

Character images primarily serve as definitive portraits.

Default framing is bust, chest-up, waist-up, or half-body. Full-body portraits are used when silhouette, posture, clothing, disability, or physicality requires them.

Backgrounds should show a recognizable but softened location connected to the subject. The environment must support the portrait without competing with the face.

Repeated images of a character preserve face, body proportions, apparent age, skin tone, hair texture, hair length, established hairstyle or silhouette, eye shape, distinguishing marks, and signature styling unless explicitly changed.

## Human Variety and Character Casting

Saint Veyre should look inhabited by distinct people rather than variations of one idealized face.

When a character description leaves physical traits unspecified, that absence creates design freedom. It should not automatically produce a young, symmetrical, conventionally attractive person with a narrow nose, large evenly spaced eyes, flawless skin, perfect teeth, and fashion-model proportions.

Explore deliberate variation across the cast in:

- Round, square, oval, long oval, rectangular, heart-shaped, triangular, and asymmetric faces.
- High, low, broad, narrow, soft, or sharply defined cheekbones.
- Wide, narrow, tall, short, sloped, or prominent foreheads.
- Broad, narrow, heavy, delicate, square, rounded, or receding jaws.
- Large, small, broad, narrow, long, short, flat, hooked, aquiline, pointed, rounded, crooked, or asymmetrical noses.
- Large, small, deep-set, protruding, hooded, monolid, upturned, downturned, close-set, wide-set, or asymmetrical eyes.
- Thick, thin, sparse, joined, uneven, highly arched, straight, or absent eyebrows.
- Large, small, protruding, close-set, pointed, rounded, uneven, or otherwise distinctive ears.
- Thin, full, wide, narrow, uneven, sharply defined, or softly shaped lips.
- Natural teeth, including unevenness, wear, staining, gaps, diastemas, missing teeth, prominent teeth, or imperfect alignment when appropriate.
- Every natural skin tone and undertone.
- Freckles, moles, scars, blemishes, acne, visible pores, birthmarks, pigmentation differences, wrinkles, sun damage, and other lived-in skin characteristics.
- Facial hair, body hair, stubble, shaved or unshaved presentation, and culturally specific grooming.
- Different ages, body compositions, heights, proportions, disabilities, and degrees of physical fitness.

Do not add unusual traits mechanically or turn difference into spectacle. Features should work together as an intentional human design rather than a checklist of eccentricities.

Ethnic and culturally specific features should be depicted naturally and respectfully. Avoid reducing ancestry to costume, stereotype, or decorative shorthand.

Not every character should be conventionally beautiful.

A character may be visually interesting because of presence, warmth, severity, intelligence, age, humour, physicality, vulnerability, strangeness, grooming, personal style, or an unforgettable individual feature.

When a character is described as beautiful, beauty should still be individualized. It need not mean symmetry, small features, flawless skin, thinness, youthfulness, or conformity to a single commercial standard.

Do not automatically beautify characters who are not described as beautiful.

Interesting design takes priority over universal glamour.

## Cast-Level Facial Distinctiveness

Before approving a new major character face, compare it with recently established characters.

Ask:

- Is the face shape already overrepresented?
- Is the same narrow nose recurring?
- Are the eyes using the same size, spacing, and tilt?
- Are the eyebrows nearly identical?
- Is the jawline repeating?
- Is the skin being smoothed to the same finish?
- Are teeth, ears, age, and grooming being ignored?
- Could this face be mistaken for another character after changing hair and clothing?

If the answer to the final question is yes, redesign the face before treating it as canonical.

Different characters may share individual traits. They should not share the same full facial architecture by default.

## Individual Visual Grammar

Each major character receives a personal visual profile defining:

- Identity anchors.
- Character-specific palette.
- Composition tendencies.
- Posture and gesture language.
- Preferred environments and props.
- Public persona.
- Shadow self or visual contradiction.
- Continuity requirements.
- Elements to avoid.

Clan does not determine palette, camera style, motif, material, or symbolism. Clan may affect an image only when relevant to that individual or scene.

## Anti-Repetition Rules

Avoid repeatedly using:

- Three-quarter portraits with crossed arms.
- Centred figures under one overhead light.
- The same blurred mansion interior.
- Red-and-black palettes as a universal vampire shorthand.
- Constant rain.
- Wine glasses as generic sophistication.
- Thrones, crowns, ravens, roses, blood, or Gothic arches without narrative purpose.
- Permanent direct eye contact.
- Routine fangs or glowing eyes.
- The same neutral expression across the cast.
- The same idealized face with different hair, clothing, skin tone, or accessories.

Before creating a portrait, vary crop, body orientation, gaze, hand visibility, camera height, light direction, background depth, foreground layering, emotional temperature, and facial architecture.

## Distinctiveness Test

Cover the subject's face and ask:

- Is the silhouette distinctive?
- Does the clothing belong specifically to this character?
- Does the posture communicate personality?
- Does the environment suggest their social world?
- Does the palette identify them?
- Could the image be reassigned to another character with minimal changes?

Then cover the hair and clothing and examine the face:

- Is the bone structure distinctive?
- Are the nose, eyes, brows, ears, mouth, jaw, and skin individually designed?
- Does the face retain identity without styling?
- Does the character resemble another cast member too closely?

If the image or face could easily belong to someone else, the visual grammar is too weak.

## Locations and City Vistas

Location images should prioritize architectural storytelling, local economic conditions, human scale, signs of current use, and conflict between preservation, neglect, and redevelopment.

Districts should differ through architecture, materials, signage, lighting, density, public activity, and weather rather than through arbitrary colour filters.

## Chapter and Session Covers

Covers should use one strong visual thesis rather than a collage of every participant. Leave deliberate room for typography. Covers may use more overt supernatural distortion than routine portraits.

## Factions and Institutions

Faction imagery should express public identity, institutional history, architecture, ritual, dress, objects of authority, spatial hierarchy, and the contradiction between stated values and actual methods.

Do not reduce faction art to a logo floating in smoke.

## Documents and Handouts

Handouts must be usable and materially credible. Paper, ink, printing method, handwriting, stamps, filing marks, folds, water damage, and age should match the document's origin.

Legibility takes priority over decorative distress.

## Objects, Clues, and Relics

Object images should function as evidence. Include clear materials, scale cues, wear, handling, discovery context, and readable important details. Alternate views may be needed for inscriptions, compartments, damage, or mechanisms.

Do not place every object on black velvet under a museum spotlight.

## Public and Storyteller Separation

Public visual files must not reveal hidden motives, secret affiliations, undiscovered transformations, or unrevealed supernatural traits. Spoiler-bearing visual profiles and prompts belong under `Storyteller/Visual/`.

## Canonical Workflow

1. Read the current character or subject file.
2. Identify stable anchors.
3. Compare the proposed facial architecture with existing major characters.
4. Create or update the visual profile.
5. Select a fresh composition using the variation matrix.
6. Generate the image.
7. Review continuity, art direction, facial distinctiveness, overall distinctiveness, and usability.
8. Treat the image as canonical only after approval.
9. Update the visual profile when a successful new trait becomes established.

Generated images do not silently overwrite established canon.
