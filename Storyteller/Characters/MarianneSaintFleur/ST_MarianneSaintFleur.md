# Marianne "Murder" Saint-Fleur

## Public Persona

Marianne Saint-Fleur is known throughout parts of Saint Veyre as a respected Gangrel with deep ties to local neighborhoods and immigrant communities.

Many Kindred view her as a street-level figure whose influence extends only so far as her personal reputation.

Her nickname, "Murder," comes from the unusually large number of crows frequently seen around her.

Most assume the birds are merely a Gangrel eccentricity.

---

## Private Truth

Marianne has spent years cultivating relationships among people who are routinely ignored by Saint Veyre's political elite.

She understands that information travels through communities long before it reaches official channels.

While she lacks formal authority, she possesses an unusually broad awareness of the city's social undercurrents.

Many influential Kindred underestimate how much she knows.

---

## Hidden Vulnerability

Marianne struggles to ignore suffering when she encounters it directly.

She can accept political compromise in theory.

In practice, watching innocent people become collateral damage is something she finds difficult to tolerate.

This makes her vulnerable to manipulation through threats directed at people she considers under her protection.

---

## Long-Term Desire

Marianne wants Saint Veyre's future to belong to more than its established powers.

She believes communities deserve stability, safety, and representation rather than serving as resources to be exploited by those above them.

She is willing to be patient.

She is not willing to surrender.

---

## Relationship To Alec

Marianne met Alec during the *Studies in Absence* exhibition at Atelier Seventeen.

Their first interaction occurred while Marianne was confronting a curator over the racial implications of an artwork titled *The Missing Heir*.

Alec approached Marianne openly and non-threateningly rather than ignoring the confrontation or approaching from behind.

Alec:

- Validated Marianne's discomfort with the artwork.
- Described the work as feeling racist and unsafe.
- Admitted her own fear of exclusion.
- Identified herself as a Black woman first.
- Privately shared her birth name, Alexandra Jean.
- Refused to silence Marianne while still offering practical alternatives.
- Helped negotiate a resolution without attaching public humiliation to Marianne's name.

Marianne now views Alec as:

- Brave
- Politically ambitious
- Socially polished
- Wealthy
- Part of elite society, but not entirely defined by it
- Willing to accept social risk rather than ignore someone in distress

Marianne respects Alec's intervention but does not yet trust her completely.

She is interested in discovering whether Alec's solidarity survives when it conflicts more directly with Alec's ambitions.

Some mutual physical interest may exist, though no romantic or personal direction has been established.

---

## Session 2 Development

Marianne attended *Studies in Absence* and objected to the presentation of *The Missing Heir*.

The artwork depicted an exclusionary social structure while placing a barely visible dark hand behind an empty chair.

A curator dismissed Marianne's interpretation as emotional.

Marianne requested:

- A revised artist's statement.
- A revised gallery note explicitly identifying racial exclusion.
- The removal of the curator from guest-facing duties.

During the private discussion, Marianne clarified that her concern regarding the curator was primarily preventative. She did not want him treating another guest in the same manner.

She accepted a compromise under which:

- The curator was removed from the floor for the evening.
- His future involvement would be reviewed later.
- The artwork's supporting text would be revised.
- Marianne would be allowed to review the new language.

The incident created a favorable first connection between Marianne and Alec.

Marianne also observed Geneviève Laurent and Camille Vauclair attempting to engage in good faith, though this does not erase her broader distrust of elite social circles.

## Storyteller Notes

Marianne should never be portrayed as anti-authority simply for the sake of rebellion.

She respects competence.

She respects responsibility.

She respects people who genuinely protect others.

Her conflicts arise when power is used carelessly, selfishly, or cruelly.

She should feel grounded, practical, and intelligent rather than ideological.
