# Camille Vauclair

## Public Persona

A sophisticated socialite and influential Toreador.

---

## Private Truth

Camille's greatest talent is understanding people rather than art.

---

## Hidden Vulnerability

She fears irrelevance more than failure.

---

## Long-Term Desire

To remain indispensable regardless of who holds formal power.

---

## Relationship To Alec

Camille met Alec during the *Studies in Absence* exhibition at Atelier Seventeen.

She initially regarded Alec as an elegant newcomer surrounded by fresh court gossip.

Alec's intervention during the conflict surrounding *The Missing Heir* significantly improved Camille's opinion of her.

Camille now considers Alec:

- Socially capable
- Politically interesting
- More principled than expected
- Skilled at mediating without publicly humiliating those involved
- Potentially useful as a bridge between social circles

Camille is curious whether Alec's intervention was an isolated act of courage or evidence of a repeatable talent.

She may test Alec through future invitations, difficult conversations, or carefully selected social problems.

Camille does not yet trust Alec.

She does, however, consider her worth cultivating.

---

## Session 2 Development

Camille hosted *Studies in Absence* at Atelier Seventeen.

After Marianne Saint-Fleur objected to the racial framing of *The Missing Heir* and to a curator's patronizing response, Camille joined a private discussion with Marianne, Alec, and Geneviève Laurent.

Camille acknowledged that:

- She selected the artwork.
- She underestimated what the work demanded from Black viewers.
- She mistook recognition for permission.
- The piece risked less from most viewers than it demanded from those it depicted.

Camille agreed to:

- Revise the artist's statement.
- Revise the gallery note to explicitly address racial exclusion.
- Remove the curator from guest-facing duties for the evening.
- Review the curator's future involvement.
- Allow Marianne to review the revised statement.

Alec's handling of the situation demonstrated judgment Camille had not expected from a newly arrived neonate.

Camille is likely to preserve several interpretations of the event rather than allow one definitive version to dominate.
